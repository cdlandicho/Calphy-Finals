Fluid Pressure

Understanding Hydrostatic Pressure and Depth

Hydrostatic pressure is the pressure applied when the fluid at rest is a result of gravity. It is caused by the mass of the fluid pushing onto a particular point in the fluid and grows with depth beneath the surface. As depth increases, the underlying fluid produces an increased downward force, causing greater pressure at the bottom.

Key Concepts

  • Hydrostatic Pressure: Pressure due to fluid weight
  • Depth Dependency: Pressure increases with depth
  • Pascal's Law: Pressure transmitted equally in all directions
  • Density Effect: Denser fluids create more pressure

Fluid Pressure

Fluid Pressure is the amount of force exerted by a fluid on a surface per unit area. It determines how strongly a fluid pushes against walls or objects in contact with it.

P Pressure (Pascal, Pa)
F Force applied (Newton, N)
A Surface area (m²)

Example

If a force of 100 N acts on an area of 2 m²:

P = 100 / 2 = 50 Pa

Hydrostatic Pressure

Hydrostatic Pressure is the pressure exerted by a fluid at rest due to the force of gravity. It increases with depth because the weight of the fluid above exerts a greater downward force on the fluid below. The deeper you go, the greater the pressure.

P Pressure (Pascal, Pa)
ρ Fluid Density (kg/m³)
g Acceleration due to Gravity (9.81 m/s²)
h Depth or Height (m)

Example

Calculate the hydrostatic pressure 10 meters below the surface of water (ρ = 1000 kg/m³):

P = 1000 × 9.81 × 10 = 98,100 Pa

Real Life Example of Pressure

Pressure in Dams

Dams are built thicker at the bottom because water pressure increases with depth, pushing harder on the lower parts of the structure.

Pressure Difference

When you suck through a straw, air pressure outside the glass pushes the liquid up into your mouth — that’s fluid pressure at work!

Pressure Application

When you push the plunger, you increase the pressure inside, forcing the liquid out through the needle.
