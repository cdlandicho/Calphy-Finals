Archimedes' Principle

The Science Behind Floating and Sinking

Archimedes’ Principle is one of the fundamental laws of fluid mechanics. It states that any object fully or partially submerged in a fluid experiences an upward force—called buoyant force— equal to the weight of the fluid it displaces. This principle helps explain why ships float, balloons rise, and icebergs only partially emerge above the water’s surface.

Did You Know?

The principle was discovered by the Greek mathematician and inventor Archimedes of Syracuse in the 3rd century BCE. Legend says he realized this while stepping into a bath and noticing the water level rise. Excited by his discovery, he ran through the streets shouting “Eureka!” (meaning “I have found it!”).

Key Concepts

  • Buoyant Force: The upward force exerted by a fluid on a submerged object.
  • Displacement: The amount of fluid pushed aside by the object.
  • Floating: Occurs when buoyant force equals or exceeds the object’s weight.
  • Sinking: Happens when the object’s weight is greater than the buoyant force.
  • Equilibrium: Achieved when both forces balance perfectly, causing the object to stay suspended in the fluid.

Ships & Submarines

Large ships float because their average density, including the air inside, is less than that of water. Submarines adjust their buoyancy by controlling the water in their ballast tanks to dive or rise.

Hot-Air Balloons

Hot air inside the balloon is less dense than the surrounding cooler air, allowing it to rise — just like objects float in water when they are less dense.

Icebergs

Ice is slightly less dense than liquid water, so only about 10% of an iceberg is visible above the surface — the rest stays submerged.

Airflow Around an Airplane Wing

Archimedes’ Principle isn’t limited to water—air acts as a fluid too. Air moves faster over the curved top of an airplane wing, creating lower pressure above and higher pressure below, producing lift.
